In the Age of AI, Why your ability to call BS is your biggest asset

Spoiler Alert: Your Greatest Edge Is Discernment

March 3, 2026

You're tired of hearing about AI, and trust me, I am too. More importantly, I'm not here to add to the noise.

Every headline, every LinkedIn post, every meeting screams at you the world is changing and you need to keep up. But I am here to say something I think gets lost in all of it: AI is not coming for the people who can think.

I've spent years in the tech industry. I've watched waves come and go. Cloud, mobile, blockchain, now AI. This time feels bigger. The speed at which AI learns, the depth it can reach, the sheer breadth of what it can process. Sorry, but no human can match that. That's just the reality.

So where does that leave us?

AI Predicts. It Doesn't Know All.

Here's the thing most people miss: AI is still a prediction engine. A remarkably powerful one, but a prediction engine nonetheless. It calculates the most probable output based on the data it's been trained on. It does not know things. It does not guarantee truth. It gets things wrong confidently, fluently, and often.

And that's exactly where you come in.

The Human Edge: Discernment

If there's one skill I'd tell anyone to sharpen right now, it's discernment. The ability to look at what AI produces and decide what's useful, and more importantly, what's flawed.

This isn't just critical thinking in the traditional sense. It's a combination of things that are deeply, stubbornly human: judgment built from lived experience, creativity that comes from making unexpected connections, ethical awareness that no model can replicate, and the emotional intelligence to understand context that data alone can't capture.

AI can draft the strategy. You decide if it's the right strategy. AI can summarize the research. You determine if the conclusion actually holds. AI can generate the answer. You ask whether it's even the right question.

That's not a small thing. That's the whole game.

The Mindset Shift

I've always believed that when a wave comes, you don't have to love it. But you do have to be honest about it. Go observe. Take what serves you. Leave what doesn't. Fighting the entire ocean alone is a waste of energy. But choosing how you move through it?

That's power.

It's about being the person in the room who knows how to use it and when not to trust it. That combination is rarer than you think.

Keep Swimming

There's no step-by-step guide to navigating this era. No "10 AI-proof careers" listicle that will actually hold up in five years. And who knows, it might not even last the list for five.

But here's what I believe: if you stay open, if you keep learning, if you sharpen your ability to discern signal from noise, you're not getting swept away. Because you're swimming.

The water's rising whether you like it or not. So you keep swimming.
